Probability theory is the language machine learning uses when data is incomplete, noisy, sampled, biased, or generated by a process we cannot observe directly. It explains why a loss function can be interpreted as likelihood, why a classifier should output calibrated uncertainty, why Bayesian reasoning updates belief rather than chasing certainty, and why modern generative models are ultimately stories about distributions. This chapter builds probability from first principles and then connects it to classification, estimation, information theory, graphical models, sampling, deep learning, and practical evaluation.

01 / LANGUAGE

Probability as the language of uncertainty in learning systems

Read this opening section as a change in mindset. Probability is not introduced because machine learning wants to be abstract; it is introduced because data rarely gives complete information. The same observation can have several possible causes, the same feature vector can support several labels, and the future case may not follow the training set exactly. The goal is therefore to make uncertainty explicit, attach it to assumptions, and update it when evidence changes.

Machine learning begins when observations do not fully determine the answer. The same image may be ambiguous, the same symptom may come from several diseases, the same user behavior may imply different intentions, and the same training set may be only a small sample from a larger population. Probability gives us a disciplined way to talk about these possibilities. It does not say the world is random in a philosophical sense; it gives a calculus for reasoning when our information is incomplete.

The important shift is to see probability as a modeling discipline rather than a bag of formulas. Every probability statement names a world of possible outcomes, the information already observed, and the assumptions that connect evidence to prediction. Once those pieces are explicit, a model's output is no longer just a number. It becomes a conditional claim about what may happen next and how strongly the data supports it. This habit makes later topics such as likelihood, calibration, and uncertainty monitoring feel like extensions of one idea rather than disconnected techniques.

A probability model starts with a sample space \(\Omega\), events \(A\subseteq\Omega\), and a function \(P\) assigning numbers to events. The axioms are simple: probabilities are nonnegative, the whole space has probability one, and disjoint events add. From these rules come almost every model we use in machine learning. The subtle part is not manipulating symbols; it is deciding what the sample space represents, what information is observed, and which assumptions connect hidden causes to visible data.

Frequentist and Bayesian readings

In a frequentist reading, probability describes long-run frequencies under repeated sampling. A classifier's error rate is the fraction of future examples it will misclassify under the data distribution. In a Bayesian reading, probability describes degrees of belief conditioned on information. A parameter can have a posterior distribution because we are uncertain about it after seeing finite data. The two views often use the same algebra but answer different questions.

Machine learning uses both viewpoints constantly. Cross-validation estimates future frequency of success; Bayesian inference represents uncertainty about parameters; calibration asks whether predicted probabilities match empirical frequencies; decision theory turns beliefs into actions. Treating these views as enemies is usually unhelpful. A good practitioner asks which uncertainty is being represented: data noise, sampling variation, parameter uncertainty, model uncertainty, or decision risk.

Data-generating processes and assumptions

A dataset is not merely a table. It is evidence produced by some process: people clicking links, sensors measuring signals, patients entering clinics, annotators labeling images, or simulators producing trajectories. A probability model is a simplified story about that process. It may say that examples are independent and identically distributed, that labels depend on features through a conditional distribution, or that hidden variables generate observations.

The assumptions matter because probability statements are conditional on them. If training data and deployment data differ, an estimated probability may fail. If labels are biased, the model can be well-calibrated to the label process but wrong about the real target. If examples are dependent, uncertainty estimates based on independence can be overconfident. Probability theory does not remove assumptions; it forces them into the open where they can be tested.

A useful habit is to write the modeling story before writing code. For a spam classifier, the story may be “messages are sampled from future email traffic, each message has a true class, and words are noisy evidence.” For a demand forecast, it may be “sales are generated by seasonality, price, promotions, and random shocks.” Such stories are simplified, but they tell us what would count as data leakage, which variables are available at prediction time, and where uncertainty should be reported.

02 / RANDOM VARIABLES

Random variables, distributions, expectation, and variance

This section moves from single uncertain events to reusable mathematical objects. A random variable lets us assign numbers to outcomes, a distribution tells us how probability is arranged over those numbers, expectation summarizes the long-run center, and variance measures instability around that center. In machine learning, these ideas appear whenever we talk about expected loss, noisy labels, stochastic gradients, confidence intervals, or the behavior of a model over future samples rather than over one example.

A random variable is a function from outcomes to values. It converts a possibly complicated event into a number, vector, class label, text token, or structured object we can model. In machine learning, features \(X\), labels \(Y\), model parameters \(\theta\), losses \(L\), and predictions \(\hat{Y}\) can all be treated as random variables. This perspective is powerful because it lets us compare algorithms through the distributions of quantities they produce.

A distribution tells us how probability mass or density is arranged. Discrete variables have probability mass functions, continuous variables have densities, and mixed objects may require more care. The notation \(p(x)\) often hides this distinction, but the idea is consistent: a distribution is a rule for assigning probability to regions of possible values. In learning, we rarely know the true distribution. We see samples and build approximations.

Expectation is an average under a distribution

The expectation of a function \(g(X)\) is the distribution-weighted average:

\[\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x)p(x)\quad\text{or}\quad \int g(x)p(x)\,dx\](1)

Many machine-learning objectives are expectations. The true risk of a predictor is \(\mathbb{E}_{(X,Y)\sim P}[\ell(f(X),Y)]\), an average loss over the unknown population distribution. The empirical risk replaces that expectation with an average over training data. Generalization is the question of when the empirical average is a trustworthy estimate of the population average.

Variance measures spread and instability

Variance is \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mathbb{E}X)^2]\). It measures how much a quantity fluctuates around its mean. In learning systems, variance appears everywhere: an estimator changes across sampled datasets, a stochastic gradient changes across mini-batches, a prediction changes across model initializations, and an online metric changes across users. Lower variance is not automatically better, because overly stable estimators can be biased.

Covariance extends variance to pairs of variables. Positive covariance means two quantities tend to move together; negative covariance means one tends to rise as the other falls. The covariance matrix of features controls PCA, Gaussian models, whitening, uncertainty ellipses, and many optimization approximations. It is the probabilistic companion to the geometry of linear algebra.

Law of large numbers and central limit theorem

The law of large numbers says sample averages converge to expectations under suitable conditions. This is why training loss, validation accuracy, and Monte Carlo estimates can be meaningful. The central limit theorem says many standardized sums approach a Gaussian distribution, explaining why normal approximations appear throughout statistics. These results justify common estimates, but they do not protect us from biased samples, dependence, heavy tails, or distribution shift.

In practice, the gap between theorem and dataset is where judgment lives. A million clicks from one recommender system are not the same as a million independent samples from the future. A small validation set can make metric changes look larger than they are. A heavy-tailed loss can make an average unstable. Probability gives the language for asking how much evidence a number really contains.

Random variables also help separate levels of variation. The individual label is random because the example may be ambiguous; the training set is random because it is sampled; the fitted model is random because it depends on the training set and optimization; the metric is random because it is computed on a finite evaluation set. When these levels are mixed together, people often confuse noise in the measurement with improvement in the model.

03 / CONDITIONING

Conditional probability, independence, and Bayes' rule

Conditional probability is the grammar of learning from evidence. A model almost never asks for the probability of an event in isolation; it asks for the probability of a label given features, a token given previous tokens, or a hidden state given observations. Independence assumptions then decide which pieces of information can be separated and which must be modeled together. Bayes' rule completes the picture by showing how prior belief and observed evidence combine into a posterior belief.

Conditional probability is the mathematics of updating information. \(P(A\mid B)\) asks how likely event \(A\) is after learning that \(B\) occurred. In machine learning, nearly every useful statement is conditional: the probability of a label given features, the probability of a token given previous tokens, the probability of a hidden cause given an observation, or the probability of failure given a subgroup. Conditioning turns raw uncertainty into context-sensitive belief.

The definition is

\[P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)},\qquad P(B)>0\](2)

This formula looks simple, but conceptually it means we restrict attention to worlds where \(B\) is true and renormalize. Conditional probability is not causal by itself. \(P(Y\mid X)\) describes association under the data distribution; it does not necessarily tell us what happens if we intervene and change \(X\). Causal reasoning needs additional assumptions beyond ordinary conditioning.

Independence and conditional independence

Events \(A\) and \(B\) are independent when \(P(A,B)=P(A)P(B)\). Random variables are independent when their joint distribution factors. Conditional independence means variables become independent after observing another variable. In models, conditional independence is gold: it reduces complexity, enables factorization, and makes inference possible. Naive Bayes, hidden Markov models, Bayesian networks, and many graphical models are built by declaring which dependencies matter and which can be ignored.

Independence assumptions are often approximations, not truths. Naive Bayes assumes features are conditionally independent given the class, which is rarely exactly correct for text or images. Yet it can work surprisingly well because the decision boundary may remain useful even when the probability model is imperfect. A probability model should be judged by the task: density estimation, calibrated prediction, ranking, classification, or causal interpretation may require different fidelity.

Bayes' rule as belief revision

Bayes' rule follows from the symmetry of joint probability:

\[P(H\mid D)=\frac{P(D\mid H)P(H)}{P(D)}\](3)

The prior \(P(H)\) describes belief before seeing data, the likelihood \(P(D\mid H)\) describes how compatible the data is with a hypothesis, the evidence \(P(D)\) normalizes across hypotheses, and the posterior \(P(H\mid D)\) is updated belief. In machine learning, \(H\) might be a class label, a parameter value, a latent cluster, a model family, or a hidden state. Bayes' rule is the common grammar of diagnosis, classification, filtering, and Bayesian inference.

The most common mistake is to confuse \(P(D\mid H)\) with \(P(H\mid D)\). A disease may make a test likely positive, while a positive test may still imply low disease probability if the disease is rare. Base rates matter. This is not a philosophical detail; it determines spam filters, medical screening, fraud detection, and any classifier deployed under class imbalance.

In supervised learning, conditioning also clarifies what the model is allowed to know. A feature available after the outcome, such as “refund issued” when predicting fraud at checkout, belongs to the wrong conditional distribution. It may create an excellent offline score and a useless deployed model. Correct probability questions include time: probability of the target given the information available at the decision moment.

04 / DISTRIBUTIONS

Common distributions and the modeling assumptions they encode

Distributions are not just formula sheets. Each one is a compact statement about what kinds of outcomes are possible and what kind of randomness we expect. A Bernoulli distribution says an event is binary, a categorical distribution says exactly one class is chosen, a Gaussian says deviations around a center are symmetric and light-tailed, and a Poisson distribution says counts arise from a rate. Choosing a distribution is therefore choosing a story about the data-generating process.

Distributions are reusable assumptions. Choosing a Bernoulli distribution says an observation is binary. Choosing a categorical distribution says exactly one class among several occurs. Choosing a Gaussian says deviations are continuous, symmetric, and summarized by mean and covariance. Choosing a Poisson says counts arise from a rate. The name of a distribution is shorthand for a story about support, noise, and variability.

Machine learning often hides these assumptions inside losses. Squared error corresponds to Gaussian observation noise with constant variance. Cross-entropy for classification corresponds to categorical likelihood. Poisson regression models count data. Logistic regression models a Bernoulli conditional probability. Understanding the distribution behind a loss helps us know when the loss is appropriate and when it will distort the problem.

Discrete distributions

Bernoulli, binomial, categorical, and multinomial distributions handle binary events, repeated binary trials, single class labels, and counts across categories. Text models use categorical distributions over vocabularies. Naive Bayes for documents may use multinomial word counts. Recommender systems may model clicks as Bernoulli events. In each case, the distribution defines what outcomes are possible and how probabilities must sum.

The categorical distribution is especially central in deep learning. A classifier maps features to logits, softmax transforms logits into class probabilities, and cross-entropy compares the predicted categorical distribution with the observed label. A language model does the same over a vocabulary at every position. Classification and next-token prediction are therefore distribution prediction problems, not just label selection.

Continuous distributions

Gaussian distributions are everywhere because they are mathematically convenient, arise as approximations to sums, and connect to least squares. A multivariate Gaussian \(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma)\) is shaped by its mean and covariance. Diagonal covariance assumes independent noise across coordinates; full covariance models correlations but requires more data. Many algorithms are easiest to understand as Gaussian assumptions plus linear algebra.

Other continuous distributions matter when Gaussian assumptions break. Exponential distributions model waiting times, Gamma distributions model positive rates or variances, Beta distributions model probabilities, Dirichlet distributions model categorical probability vectors, and Student-\(t\) distributions handle heavier tails. Choosing a distribution is a way of respecting constraints: probabilities must stay between zero and one, variances must be positive, and counts cannot be negative.

Exponential family and sufficient statistics

Many useful distributions belong to the exponential family:

\[p(x\mid\eta)=h(x)\exp\left(\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\right)\](4)

Here \(T(x)\) are sufficient statistics, \(\eta\) are natural parameters, and \(A(\eta)\) is the log-partition function that normalizes the distribution. This form unifies Bernoulli, categorical, Gaussian, Poisson, Gamma, and many others. It also explains why gradients of log likelihood often compare observed statistics with model-expected statistics.

Distribution choice should follow the object being modeled. A rating bounded between one and five should not automatically be treated like an unconstrained Gaussian variable. A waiting time should not be predicted with a model that can produce negative values. A probability vector should live on a simplex. Many bad models begin by ignoring support constraints, then try to repair impossible predictions after the fact.

05 / ESTIMATION

Likelihood, maximum likelihood, MAP, and Bayesian posterior inference

Estimation is where probability becomes training. We propose a model family, observe data, and ask which parameters make those observations plausible. Maximum likelihood turns that question into an objective function; MAP adds a prior preference; Bayesian inference keeps a distribution over plausible parameters instead of choosing only one. The practical lesson is that many losses used in machine learning are not arbitrary penalties. They come from probabilistic assumptions about how the data could have been generated.

Estimation asks how to learn unknown quantities from data. Suppose data \(D=\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n\) is modeled by \(p_\theta(x)\). The likelihood \(p_\theta(D)\) treats the data as fixed and parameters as the variable being evaluated. If examples are independent, likelihood factors:

\[\mathcal{L}(\theta;D)=\prod_{i=1}^n p_\theta(x_i),\qquad \ell(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\log p_\theta(x_i)\](5)

We usually optimize log likelihood because products of many small probabilities underflow and because sums are easier to differentiate. Maximum likelihood estimation chooses the parameter that makes observed data most probable under the model. Many learning algorithms can be read as MLE with a particular probability model.

MLE connects probability to loss functions

Linear regression with squared error is MLE under Gaussian noise. Logistic regression is MLE under Bernoulli labels with a logistic link. Softmax classification is MLE under categorical labels. Language-model pre-training is MLE under an autoregressive categorical distribution. This connection is powerful because it turns arbitrary-looking losses into assumptions about how data was generated.

MLE can be statistically efficient when the model is correct and data is abundant, but it can overfit when parameters are flexible. A model can assign high likelihood to training data while generalizing poorly. Regularization, priors, validation, early stopping, and data augmentation all modify the estimation problem so that fitting observed data does not become memorizing observed accidents.

MAP estimation adds a prior

Maximum a posteriori estimation chooses the parameter with highest posterior probability:

\[\theta_{\text{MAP}}=\arg\max_\theta\left[\log p(D\mid\theta)+\log p(\theta)\right]\](6)

The prior \(p(\theta)\) encodes preference before data. A Gaussian prior on weights corresponds to \(L_2\) regularization; a Laplace prior corresponds to \(L_1\)-like sparsity pressure. MAP is Bayesian in form but still returns a point estimate. Full Bayesian inference keeps the posterior distribution, representing uncertainty over parameters rather than committing to a single value.

Posterior predictive distributions

The Bayesian predictive distribution averages predictions over posterior uncertainty:

\[p(y_\ast\mid x_\ast,D)=\int p(y_\ast\mid x_\ast,\theta)p(\theta\mid D)\,d\theta\](7)

This formula is beautiful and often intractable. Exact integration is possible for special conjugate models but difficult for deep networks. Approximate inference, sampling, variational methods, Laplace approximations, ensembles, and dropout-style approximations all try to capture some of this uncertainty. The goal is not only to predict a label, but to know how much the system should trust itself.

Estimation should therefore be read as a pipeline: choose a probabilistic story, collect data under a sampling process, define a likelihood, add regularization or priors, optimize or infer, and then check predictive behavior on data that mimics deployment. If any step is mismatched, the final parameter estimate may look precise while answering the wrong question. Probability keeps the estimate tied to the data story that produced it.

06 / CLASSIFICATION

Bayes classifiers, generative models, discriminative models, and calibration

Classification becomes clearer once we separate three tasks: estimating probabilities, choosing a label, and taking an action. The Bayes classifier describes the ideal decision rule when posterior probabilities and costs are known. Generative classifiers model how each class could produce the features; discriminative classifiers model the boundary or posterior directly; calibration asks whether the reported probabilities can be trusted as frequencies. Real systems need all three viewpoints because a high-scoring label is not automatically a good decision.

Classification is a decision problem under uncertainty. We observe features \(x\) and choose a label \(y\). The ideal classifier under zero-one loss chooses the class with highest posterior probability:

\[\hat{y}(x)=\arg\max_y P(Y=y\mid X=x)\](8)

This is the Bayes classifier. It is optimal if the true posterior is known and the cost of every mistake is equal. Real models approximate it from finite data. When mistakes have unequal costs, decision theory replaces the maximum posterior rule with minimum expected loss. In medicine, fraud, moderation, and safety systems, thresholds should reflect cost, not only accuracy.

Geometrically, the Bayes classifier partitions the feature space into regions where different posterior probabilities dominate. The boundary between two classes is the set of points where their posterior probabilities are equal. If the class-conditional distributions are Gaussian with shared covariance, this boundary is linear; if covariances differ, it becomes quadratic. Neural classifiers can create much more flexible boundaries, but they are still trying to approximate the same posterior ordering: which label should receive most probability at this location in feature space?

The prior \(P(Y=y)\) controls the baseline mass of each class before observing features. This matters whenever classes are imbalanced. If a disease is rare, even a suspicious symptom pattern must overcome a small prior; if fraud is rare, a classifier can achieve high accuracy by predicting "not fraud" too often. Priors are not merely philosophical. They determine how evidence is converted into posterior odds, and changing the deployment population changes the correct prior even when \(p(x\mid y)\) stays similar.

The Bayes classifier also separates ranking from action. A model may rank cases well while its probabilities are poorly calibrated; it may classify well under one threshold and fail under another. For this reason practical classification should report not only accuracy but confusion matrices, ROC/PR curves, calibration error, subgroup metrics, and threshold-specific expected cost. Probability turns classification from a single score into a decision surface that can be inspected, questioned, and adapted to the real consequences of mistakes.

Naive Bayes and the value of a wrong assumption

Naive Bayes uses Bayes' rule and assumes features are conditionally independent given the class:

\[P(Y=y\mid x_1,\ldots,x_d)\propto P(Y=y)\prod_{j=1}^{d}P(x_j\mid Y=y)\](9)

The assumption is usually false, especially for text where words are correlated. Yet the method can work well because it estimates fewer parameters and often ranks classes correctly. It is a lesson in modeling: a simplified probability model may be poor as a literal description of the world and still useful for decisions. The danger is overinterpreting its probabilities as calibrated truth.

Generative versus discriminative classifiers

A generative classifier models \(p(x\mid y)\) and \(p(y)\), then uses Bayes' rule to obtain \(p(y\mid x)\). A discriminative classifier models \(p(y\mid x)\) directly or learns a decision boundary without modeling feature generation. Naive Bayes and Gaussian discriminant analysis are generative; logistic regression and many neural classifiers are discriminative. Generative models can use unlabeled structure and handle missing data naturally, while discriminative models often achieve better classification when enough labeled data is available.

The distinction is not absolute. Modern systems often combine both: a generative language model may produce candidate answers, a discriminative reranker may score them, and a calibrated classifier may decide whether to abstain. The probabilistic question is always the same: which distribution is being modeled, and is that distribution the one needed for the decision?

Calibration and decision thresholds

A model is calibrated if, among examples assigned probability 0.8, roughly 80 percent are correct. Calibration differs from accuracy. A highly accurate model can be overconfident, and a less accurate model can express uncertainty honestly. Calibration matters when probabilities drive triage, ranking, risk scores, or human review. Temperature scaling, isotonic regression, Platt scaling, and validation-based threshold tuning are practical tools.

Class imbalance makes calibration and thresholds especially important. In rare-event detection, a high accuracy can be meaningless because always predicting the majority class already looks good. Precision, recall, ROC curves, precision-recall curves, expected cost, and subgroup calibration provide a fuller picture. Probability is valuable because it lets us separate scoring from decision making: estimate risk first, then choose an action using the right cost model.

Bayes classifiers also show why the same score can require different actions in different products. A 5 percent fraud probability may be enough to request extra verification but not enough to block a payment. A 5 percent disease probability may require another test if the treatment is cheap and the disease is serious. The posterior probability is only an input to the decision; the loss function, available interventions, and human consequences complete the rule.

07 / INFORMATION

Entropy, cross-entropy, KL divergence, and mutual information

Information theory gives language for comparing uncertainty before and after a model makes a prediction. Entropy measures how spread out a distribution is, cross-entropy measures how costly it is to predict with the wrong distribution, KL divergence measures mismatch between two distributions, and mutual information measures how much one variable reduces uncertainty about another. These quantities are everywhere in ML because learning is often the process of moving probability mass toward better explanations of the data.

Information theory measures uncertainty and distribution mismatch. Entropy quantifies the average surprise of a random variable:

\[H(P)=-\sum_x P(x)\log P(x)\](10)

A deterministic variable has zero entropy because there is no surprise. A uniform distribution over many outcomes has high entropy because many outcomes are plausible. In machine learning, entropy appears in decision trees, active learning, exploration, regularization, generative modeling, and uncertainty visualization.

Cross-entropy as prediction loss

Cross-entropy between a true distribution \(P\) and a model distribution \(Q\) is \(H(P,Q)=-\mathbb{E}_{x\sim P}\log Q(x)\). Classification loss is cross-entropy where the observed label is treated as a one-hot empirical distribution. Language modeling loss is cross-entropy at every token. Minimizing cross-entropy encourages the model to place high probability on observed outcomes, but it also penalizes overconfidence on the wrong outcome strongly.

Cross-entropy is not merely a convenient loss. It is the number of extra nats or bits needed when encoding data from \(P\) using a code optimized for \(Q\). This compression view explains why predictive modeling and representation learning are connected. A model that assigns shorter codes to real data has captured structure in the data-generating process.

KL divergence and distribution mismatch

KL divergence is

\[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q)=\sum_x P(x)\log\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\](11)

It measures how costly it is to use \(Q\) when data comes from \(P\). KL is nonnegative but not symmetric, so the direction matters. Variational inference, diffusion models, RL policy updates, language-model alignment, and domain adaptation all use KL-like penalties. Different directions produce different behavior: one may cover all modes, another may focus on one high-density mode.

Mutual information and useful representation

Mutual information measures how much knowing one variable reduces uncertainty about another. A feature representation should retain information about the target while discarding nuisance variation. This idea appears in representation learning, contrastive learning, feature selection, and information bottleneck methods. The challenge is that mutual information is hard to estimate reliably in high dimensions, so practical algorithms use bounds and proxies.

Information-theoretic language is powerful, but it can become vague if detached from estimators and assumptions. When someone says a model “maximizes information,” ask which variables, which distribution, which estimator, and which bound. Otherwise, the phrase may hide more than it explains. Good probability practice keeps the measurable quantity visible.

Information theory also explains regularization intuitively. A representation that preserves every detail of the input may memorize nuisance variation; a representation that discards too much cannot predict the target. Learning often searches for a compression that keeps the useful bits and throws away irrelevant ones. This is why noise injection, bottlenecks, contrastive objectives, and minimum description length ideas keep reappearing under different names.

08 / GRAPHICAL MODELS

Graphical models, latent variables, and probabilistic structure

This section shows how probability handles many variables without losing the shape of the problem. A graphical model is a map of dependence assumptions: it says which variables directly influence which others and which relationships become independent after conditioning. Latent variables add hidden causes that make observed patterns easier to explain. The purpose is not to draw pretty diagrams, but to make assumptions explicit enough that inference, learning, and criticism become possible.

Graphical models use graphs to express factorization and conditional independence. Nodes are random variables; edges describe direct probabilistic dependence under the model. A directed Bayesian network factors a joint distribution into local conditionals:

\[p(x_1,\ldots,x_d)=\prod_{j=1}^{d}p(x_j\mid \operatorname{pa}(x_j))\](12)

This factorization can turn an impossible joint distribution into manageable pieces. It also makes assumptions explicit. If a node has only a few parents, the model says that all other variables influence it only through those parents. This is the probabilistic counterpart of modular design.

Latent variables explain hidden structure

Latent variables are unobserved causes or summaries used to explain observed data. A mixture model uses a hidden cluster assignment; topic models use hidden topic proportions; hidden Markov models use hidden states; factor analysis uses hidden continuous factors. Latent variables are useful because they let a model explain complicated observations through simpler hidden structure, but they also introduce identifiability and inference challenges.

In machine learning, latent variables often act like compressed explanations. A cluster label summarizes a group, a representation vector summarizes an input, and a state variable summarizes history. The probabilistic question is whether the latent variable has a clear generative role, whether it is identifiable from data, and whether downstream decisions require interpreting it or only using it predictively.

EM algorithm: alternating between hidden assignments and parameters

The Expectation-Maximization algorithm handles models with latent variables by alternating two steps. The E-step computes or approximates the posterior over hidden variables under current parameters. The M-step updates parameters to maximize expected complete-data log likelihood. For Gaussian mixture models, this means estimating soft cluster memberships, then updating cluster means, covariances, and weights.

EM is not magic and not guaranteed to find the global optimum. It can get stuck in local optima, depend on initialization, and overfit with too many components. Its value is conceptual: when data has hidden structure, learning often alternates between explaining observations with hidden variables and improving the explanation model. Variational inference and many modern latent models generalize this idea.

Probabilistic programming and model transparency

Probabilistic programming languages let users write generative models and delegate inference to algorithms. This separates model specification from inference machinery. Such tools are useful when domain assumptions are important: epidemiology, forecasting, causal modeling, scientific measurement, and decision support. They make uncertainty explicit and inspectable, but still require careful checking of priors, likelihoods, convergence, and model fit.

Deep learning often learns representations automatically, while graphical models ask us to specify structure. The two are complementary. A neural network can parameterize a conditional distribution inside a graphical model; a probabilistic model can impose structure around neural components. The frontier is not choosing one worldview, but combining flexible function approximation with explicit uncertainty and dependency structure.

Graphical thinking is especially useful for debugging leakage and spurious correlation. Drawing nodes for data source, annotation process, user group, time, feature, label, and prediction often reveals paths that should not exist. If a hospital ID predicts disease because one hospital serves sicker patients, the graph helps distinguish useful medical signal from deployment-fragile shortcut. The picture does not solve the problem, but it makes the assumption visible.

09 / INFERENCE

Sampling, Monte Carlo, MCMC, and variational inference

Many probabilistic questions cannot be answered by closed-form algebra. Sampling methods answer by simulation: draw many possible worlds and average their behavior. MCMC constructs samples from difficult distributions by walking through the space in a carefully designed way. Variational inference answers differently: replace the difficult distribution with a simpler one and optimize the approximation. Both strategies are essential because modern ML often needs uncertainty estimates even when exact inference is impossible.

Many probabilistic questions require expectations or posterior distributions that cannot be computed exactly. The integral may be high-dimensional, the normalizing constant unknown, or the model too complex. Approximate inference is the art of replacing impossible exact computation with controlled approximations. Machine learning uses these methods for Bayesian models, generative models, reinforcement learning, uncertainty estimation, and simulation.

Monte Carlo methods approximate expectations using samples:

\[\mathbb{E}_{p}[f(X)]\approx \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}f(x^{(s)}),\qquad x^{(s)}\sim p\](13)

The approximation error decreases like \(1/\sqrt{S}\) under broad conditions, independent of dimension in a formal sense, though getting good samples can become very hard in high dimensions. This is why sampling quality, not just sample count, matters. Correlated or biased samples can make an estimate look precise while being wrong.

MCMC samples from distributions known up to a constant

Markov Chain Monte Carlo constructs a chain whose stationary distribution is the target posterior. It is useful when we can evaluate an unnormalized density but cannot compute the normalizing constant. Metropolis-Hastings proposes moves and accepts them with a probability that corrects for the proposal. Hamiltonian Monte Carlo uses gradient information to move efficiently through continuous parameter spaces.

MCMC diagnostics matter. A chain may mix slowly, remain trapped in one mode, or appear stable while missing important regions. Multiple chains, effective sample size, trace plots, and domain checks help. The central lesson is that sampling is computation with uncertainty. The output is not a single number but an approximation whose reliability must itself be assessed.

Variational inference turns inference into optimization

Variational inference chooses an approximating family \(q_\phi(z)\) and finds the member closest to the true posterior, often by maximizing an evidence lower bound. It is usually faster and more scalable than MCMC, but introduces approximation bias. The approximating family may be too simple and underestimate uncertainty. In deep learning, VAEs, amortized inference, and many latent variable models rely on variational ideas.

The tradeoff between MCMC and variational inference mirrors a broader pattern. Sampling may be more faithful but expensive; optimization may be scalable but biased. A practical ML system often chooses the approximation that is good enough for the decision being made. If uncertainty drives medical triage or scientific claims, approximation quality matters more than if uncertainty only ranks low-risk recommendations.

Simulation is another form of probabilistic thinking. Before deploying a model, we can simulate label noise, missing data, class imbalance, drift, delayed feedback, or rare events, then observe how the training pipeline responds. A simulation is not proof that reality will behave the same way, but it is a controlled laboratory for finding which assumptions the system silently depends on.

10 / GENERATION

Generative models: from mixture models to VAEs, autoregression, and diffusion

A generative model is a probability model that assigns mass to possible data, not only to labels. This changes the question from "what class is this?" to "what kinds of objects could exist, and how likely are they?" Mixture models combine simple components, VAEs introduce continuous latent causes, autoregressive models generate one piece at a time, and diffusion models learn to reverse noise. Different architectures choose different compromises between likelihood, sample quality, controllability, and computational tractability.

Generative modeling asks for a distribution over data. A classifier estimates \(p(y\mid x)\); a generative model tries to represent \(p(x)\), \(p(x,y)\), or a conditional distribution such as \(p(x\mid c)\). This allows sampling, imputation, denoising, simulation, compression, anomaly detection, and conditional creation. The model is judged not only by whether it predicts labels, but by whether it captures the structure of the data itself.

Older generative models include Gaussian mixtures, hidden Markov models, topic models, and factor analysis. They are still valuable because their assumptions are explicit and inference can be interpretable. Modern neural generative models use deep networks to parameterize distributions while retaining probabilistic objectives. The vocabulary changed, but the central question remains: how is probability mass assigned to possible observations?

Autoregressive models decompose a joint distribution using the chain rule: \(p(x_{1:T})=\prod_t p(x_t\mid x_{

The autoregressive view explains why LLMs are probabilistic generators. They do not retrieve a fixed answer from a table; they repeatedly sample from conditional distributions. Decoding settings such as temperature and top-\(p\) sampling modify how those probabilities are turned into text. Reliability therefore depends on the learned distribution, the prompt, and the sampling policy.

VAEs and latent-variable generation

Variational autoencoders introduce a latent variable \(z\), a decoder \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\), and an approximate encoder \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\). Training maximizes a lower bound balancing reconstruction quality with a KL penalty that keeps latent codes near a prior. The result is a smooth latent space from which new samples can be generated. VAEs make the probability model explicit, though samples may be blurrier than adversarial or diffusion models in some domains.

Latent variables are useful when observations have hidden factors: style, topic, pose, speaker, cluster, or environment. A good latent model separates meaningful variation from noise. But disentanglement is not guaranteed by wishing for it. It depends on architecture, objective, supervision, inductive biases, and data. Probability tells us what is being optimized, not which human concept the latent axes must represent.

Diffusion models as learned denoising processes

Diffusion models gradually corrupt data with noise, then train a neural network to reverse the process. Generation starts from noise and repeatedly denoises. Probabilistically, the model learns transitions that move from an easy prior distribution toward the data distribution. This framework has become central in image, audio, video, and increasingly multimodal generation.

Diffusion highlights a recurring theme: complex distributions can be learned by breaking them into many simpler conditional steps. Autoregressive models generate token by token; diffusion models denoise step by step; normalizing flows transform simple distributions through invertible maps. Different generative families choose different ways to make probability computation tractable.

Generative models should be evaluated according to purpose. If the goal is realistic images, perceptual quality and diversity matter. If the goal is anomaly detection, likelihood ranking and false alarms matter. If the goal is scientific simulation, calibrated uncertainty and conservation laws may matter more than visual appeal. A model can produce impressive samples while assigning poor probabilities, or good likelihood while generating dull samples. The distributional question must match the use case.

11 / UNCERTAINTY

Uncertainty in deep learning: aleatoric, epistemic, ensembles, and conformal prediction

Deep networks often output confident numbers, but confidence is not the same as trustworthy uncertainty. Aleatoric uncertainty comes from noise that remains even with perfect knowledge; epistemic uncertainty comes from limited data or model uncertainty. Ensembles approximate the idea of several plausible models, and conformal prediction provides coverage-style guarantees with fewer modeling assumptions. The practical question is when the system should predict, when it should widen its answer, and when it should ask for help.

Deep networks often output probabilities, but a softmax score is not automatically a trustworthy uncertainty estimate. The model may be confident on out-of-distribution inputs, adversarial examples, corrupted sensors, or groups underrepresented in training. Probability in deep learning therefore has two jobs: define training objectives and express uncertainty in deployment. The second job is harder because it requires knowing what the model does not know.

Aleatoric uncertainty is irreducible noise in the data: a blurry image, random user behavior, or genuinely ambiguous label. Epistemic uncertainty is uncertainty about the model due to limited data or insufficient knowledge. More data can reduce epistemic uncertainty but not aleatoric uncertainty. Separating them matters. If uncertainty is aleatoric, the system may need human review or better sensors; if epistemic, it may need more data in that region.

Ensembles and approximate Bayesian thinking

Ensembles train multiple models and compare their predictions. Agreement suggests stability; disagreement signals uncertainty. Deep ensembles are simple and often strong, though expensive. Dropout at inference, Laplace approximations, stochastic weight averaging, and Bayesian neural networks all try to represent parameter uncertainty more directly or cheaply. None is perfect, but each is a tool for moving beyond a single overconfident prediction.

The practical question is calibration under the deployment distribution. An ensemble calibrated on a validation set may still be miscalibrated after a domain shift. Monitoring must track not only average accuracy but confidence, subgroup performance, drift, and abstention behavior. A probability output is a promise about frequency; production data tests whether the promise is kept.

Conformal prediction gives distribution-light coverage guarantees

Conformal prediction builds prediction sets with coverage guarantees under exchangeability. For a classifier, instead of returning one label, it may return a set of plausible labels. The set is small when the model is confident and larger when uncertain. The guarantee is about long-run coverage, not whether every individual set contains the truth. It is attractive because it wraps around many existing models.

Like all guarantees, conformal prediction depends on assumptions. If calibration data and future data are not exchangeable, coverage can fail. If groups differ, average coverage can hide poor subgroup coverage. Still, the method embodies a valuable probabilistic habit: when a single prediction is not reliable enough, return a structured uncertainty statement that downstream decisions can use.

Out-of-distribution detection and abstention

A model trained on one distribution may face another. OOD detection tries to identify inputs that differ from training data enough that predictions should be distrusted. Methods include density scores, feature-space distances, energy scores, ensembles, reconstruction errors, and specialized detectors. The difficulty is that high-dimensional density and semantic novelty are not the same: a strange-looking input may be harmless, while a natural-looking input may be outside the task.

Abstention is a decision, not an embarrassment. A system that knows when to defer can be safer than one that always answers. Probability supports abstention by estimating confidence, expected cost, and value of information. In high-stakes ML, the best action may be to ask for more data, call a tool, route to a human, or decline to decide.

Uncertainty estimates must also be communicated. A user may not know how to interpret a score of 0.73, but may understand “high risk, needs review” or “prediction set contains three plausible labels.” The interface should preserve probabilistic meaning without pretending that the number is more precise than the data supports. Good uncertainty design is both statistical and human.

12 / PRACTICE

Evaluation, leakage, fairness, and a practical probability checklist

The final section turns probability into an engineering discipline. A probabilistic model should not be judged only by accuracy, because deployment decisions depend on calibration, uncertainty, subgroup behavior, distribution shift, and the costs of different mistakes. Evaluation asks whether the probability statements made during training still mean the same thing in the future environment. This is where leakage, fairness, monitoring, and decision thresholds become part of the same probabilistic workflow.

Probability becomes useful when it changes how we evaluate systems. Accuracy alone rarely tells enough. We need uncertainty intervals, calibration curves, cost-sensitive metrics, subgroup metrics, drift monitoring, and error analysis. A model is deployed into a stream of future cases, not into the training spreadsheet. Evaluation should approximate the decisions the system will support and the population it will face.

Data leakage is a probabilistic failure: information from the future, validation set, or target leaks into training features. The result is an evaluation distribution easier than deployment. Leakage can enter through preprocessing, duplicate records, time windows, target-derived features, user-level splits, or global statistics. The cure is to define the prediction time, available information, sampling unit, and split rule before modeling.

Probability across the machine-learning workflow

A complete machine-learning project can be read as a chain of conditional probability questions. Data collection asks which population the sample represents: \(p_{\text{train}}(x,y)\) is never the world itself, only the part of the world made visible by instruments, logging rules, incentives, missingness, and selection. Feature construction asks which variables are observed before the decision and which variables leak future information. Modeling asks which conditional distribution is useful enough: \(p(y\mid x)\) for prediction, \(p(x,y)\) for generation and diagnosis, or \(p(z\mid x)\) when hidden structure must be inferred. Training asks which criterion makes the proposed distribution close to the empirical evidence, and deployment asks whether the same conditional relationships survive outside the dataset.

This view explains why probability is not an isolated prerequisite but the grammar of the whole system. A regression model usually estimates a conditional mean, \(E[Y\mid X=x]\), and hides assumptions about noise around that mean. A classifier estimates or approximates class probabilities and later turns them into actions through thresholds. A recommender estimates uncertain preferences from sparse observations. A reinforcement-learning policy samples actions from a state-conditioned distribution and updates it using delayed rewards. A language model estimates a next-token distribution, then decoding transforms that distribution into one concrete text. Different fields appear different because their objects differ, but the repeated pattern is the same: observe partial evidence, represent uncertainty, update beliefs, and choose an action under cost.

Geometry and probability also meet in a concrete way. A distribution over vectors is a cloud of possible points; its mean is the center of mass, its covariance ellipsoid describes preferred directions of variation, and a density contour marks regions of equal plausibility. Learning changes this geometry. PCA rotates the coordinate system toward directions with high variance. Gaussian discriminant analysis compares class ellipsoids. Logistic regression bends a linear score into a probability through the sigmoid. Neural networks repeatedly transform the data cloud until the final representation makes useful conditional probabilities easier to express. Thus the physical picture is not only randomness; it is mass moving through representation space.

In training, probabilistic assumptions become gradients. The negative log-likelihood says that examples assigned low probability exert large corrective force. Cross-entropy pushes probability mass toward the observed label. KL divergence penalizes one distribution for placing mass where another says little should exist. Variational inference and diffusion training optimize bounds or denoising losses that are probabilistic in origin but implemented as ordinary differentiable objectives. This is why probability and optimization cannot be cleanly separated in modern ML: probability defines what should be true, and optimization supplies the path by which parameters are moved.

Probability also clarifies the difference between prediction and decision. The probability \(0.8\) is not automatically an action; it becomes an action only after we specify utilities, costs, constraints, and the value of more information. In medical triage, a low threshold may be justified because missing a dangerous case is costly. In content ranking, calibration and long-term feedback may matter more than single-step accuracy. In fraud detection, a prediction may trigger human review rather than an automatic rejection. Decision theory makes this explicit: choose the action \(a\) that minimizes expected risk \(E[L(a,Y)\mid x]\), not merely the label with the largest probability.

Finally, deployment turns every probability model into a monitoring problem. The training distribution is historical, while the deployment distribution is alive. Users change behavior, data pipelines change, sensors drift, policies alter incentives, and the model's own decisions can reshape future data. A probabilistic ML system therefore needs drift checks, calibration monitoring, uncertainty summaries, subgroup analysis, and human escalation paths. The practical question is not whether the model once fit a dataset, but under which conditions its probability statements are still trustworthy enough for the decisions attached to them.

Fairness and subgroup uncertainty

A model can have good average performance and poor performance for a subgroup. Probability helps reveal this by estimating metrics with uncertainty for each group and by checking calibration separately. However, fairness criteria can conflict: equalized odds, demographic parity, predictive parity, and calibration may not all hold simultaneously. The right criterion depends on the decision context and social cost, not only mathematics.

Small subgroups create wide uncertainty intervals. A measured gap may be noisy, while a real gap may be hidden by insufficient data. Responsible evaluation reports both estimates and uncertainty. It also examines data collection, label quality, historical bias, and feedback loops. Probability cannot decide values for us, but it can prevent false precision.

A learning map for probability and ML

A durable path begins with sample spaces, random variables, expectation, variance, conditioning, Bayes' rule, and common distributions. The central habit

Whenever a model produces a number, ask: probability of what, under which conditioning information, according to which model, estimated from which data, and used for which decision? This single question turns probability from a chapter of mathematics into an engineering discipline for learning systems.

A practical study routine is to build tiny experiments. Simulate biased sampling and watch accuracy fail. Train naive Bayes and logistic regression on the same text data and compare calibration. Fit a Gaussian mixture and inspect EM sensitivity to initialization. Estimate an expectation by Monte Carlo and plot error versus sample count. Calibrate a neural classifier with temperature scaling. These exercises make probability visible as behavior, not only notation.

01 / 语言

概率是学习系统描述不确定性的语言

阅读开篇时，先把它看成一次思维方式的转换。概率论并不是因为机器学习想显得抽象才被引入，而是因为数据很少给出完整信息。同一个观察可能有多种原因，同一个特征向量可能支持多个标签，未来样本也未必严格服从训练集。因此我们的目标是把不确定性显式表达出来，把它绑定到假设上，并在证据变化时更新它。

机器学习之所以需要概率，是因为观察通常不能唯一决定答案。同一张图片可能含糊，同一种症状可能来自多种疾病，同一段用户行为可能代表不同意图，同一个训练集也只是更大总体中的一个样本。概率给我们一套有纪律的方式讨论这些可能性。它并不一定宣称世界在哲学意义上随机，而是在信息不完整时提供一套推理演算。

最重要的转变，是把概率看成一种建模纪律，而不是一袋公式。每一个概率陈述都在说明可能结果构成的世界、已经观察到的信息，以及把证据连接到预测的假设。只要这些部分被说清楚，模型输出就不再只是一个数字，而是一个关于“接下来可能发生什么、数据在多大程度上支持它”的条件化主张。这个习惯会让后面的似然、校准和不确定性监控都显得像同一个思想的自然延伸，而不是彼此孤立的技巧。

一个概率模型从样本空间 \(\Omega\)、事件 \(A\subseteq\Omega\) 和概率函数 \(P\) 开始。公理很简单：概率非负，整个空间概率为一，互斥事件概率可加。机器学习中的大多数模型都能从这些规则展开。真正困难的不是符号运算，而是决定样本空间代表什么、哪些信息被观察到、以及哪些假设把隐藏原因与可见数据连接起来。

频率派与贝叶斯解释

在频率派解释中，概率描述重复抽样下的长期频率。分类器的错误率，就是未来样本中被错分的比例。在贝叶斯解释中，概率描述给定信息后的信念程度。参数可以拥有后验分布，因为有限数据之后我们仍然不确定它。两种观点常使用同一套代数，却回答不同问题。

机器学习实际上同时使用这两种视角。交叉验证估计未来成功频率；贝叶斯推断表示参数不确定性；校准检验预测概率是否匹配经验频率；决策论把信念转化为行动。把两种观点当成敌人通常没有帮助。更好的做法是追问：当前表示的是哪一种不确定性，数据噪声、抽样波动、参数不确定性、模型不确定性，还是决策风险？

数据生成过程与假设

数据集不只是表格，而是某个过程留下的证据：用户点击链接，传感器测量信号，患者进入诊所，标注员给图片贴标签，模拟器生成轨迹。概率模型是关于这个过程的简化故事。它可能假设样本独立同分布，标签通过某个条件分布依赖特征，或者隐藏变量先产生观察。

假设之所以重要，是因为概率陈述都以假设为条件。如果训练数据和部署数据不同，估计出的概率可能失效；如果标签本身有偏，模型可能对标签过程校准，却偏离真实目标；如果样本之间相关，却按独立样本计算不确定性，就会过度自信。概率论不会消除假设，它会把假设推到明处，让我们可以检验。

一个非常实用的习惯，是先写建模故事，再写代码。对垃圾邮件分类器，故事可以是“邮件来自未来流量，每封邮件有真实类别，词语是有噪证据”；对需求预测，故事可以是“销量由季节、价格、促销和随机冲击共同产生”。这些故事当然是简化的，但它会告诉我们什么算数据泄漏，哪些变量在预测时可用，以及不确定性应在哪里报告。

02 / 随机变量

随机变量、分布、期望与方差

这一节把单个不确定事件推进为可反复使用的数学对象。随机变量让我们给结果赋数值，分布说明概率如何安排在这些数值上，期望总结长期中心，方差衡量围绕中心的波动。在机器学习中，只要讨论期望损失、噪声标签、随机梯度、置信区间，或模型在未来样本流上的表现，而不只是某一个样本上的表现，就会用到这些概念。

随机变量是从结果到数值或对象的函数。它把复杂事件转化成我们可以建模的数字、向量、类别、文本 token 或结构化对象。在机器学习中，特征 \(X\)、标签 \(Y\)、模型参数 \(\theta\)、损失 \(L\)、预测 \(\hat{Y}\) 都可以看作随机变量。这个视角很强，因为它让我们能通过算法产生的量的分布来比较算法。

分布告诉我们概率质量或密度如何安排。离散变量有概率质量函数，连续变量有密度，混合对象则需要更谨慎。记号 \(p(x)\) 经常隐藏这些差别，但核心一致：分布是给可能值区域分配概率的规则。在学习中，我们几乎从不知道真实分布。我们看到样本，并构建近似。

期望是分布下的平均

函数 \(g(X)\) 的期望是按分布加权的平均：

\[\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x)p(x)\quad\text{or}\quad \int g(x)p(x)\,dx\](1)

许多机器学习目标都是期望。预测器的真实风险是 \(\mathbb{E}_{(X,Y)\sim P}[\ell(f(X),Y)]\)，即未知总体分布上的平均损失。经验风险用训练数据平均替代这个期望。泛化问题本质上就是：什么时候经验平均可以可信地估计总体平均？

方差衡量波动与不稳定

方差是 \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=\mathbb{E}[(X-\mathbb{E}X)^2]\)，衡量一个量围绕均值波动多少。在学习系统中，方差无处不在：估计量会随抽样数据集变化，随机梯度会随 mini-batch 变化，预测会随模型初始化变化，在线指标会随用户样本变化。低方差不必然更好，因为过于稳定的估计可能带有偏差。

协方差把方差扩展到变量对。正协方差表示两个量倾向一起升降，负协方差表示一个升高时另一个倾向降低。特征协方差矩阵控制 PCA、高斯模型、白化、不确定性椭圆和许多优化近似。它是线性代数几何视角的概率伙伴。

大数定律与中心极限定理

大数定律说，在合适条件下，样本平均会收敛到期望。这就是训练损失、验证准确率和 Monte Carlo 估计有意义的原因。中心极限定理说，许多标准化后的和会接近高斯分布，解释了为什么正态近似在统计中频繁出现。这些结果支撑常见估计，但不能保护我们免受有偏样本、依赖关系、重尾分布或分布漂移的影响。

实践中，定理和数据集之间的缝隙正是判断力所在。来自同一个推荐系统的一百万次点击，不等于未来总体中的一百万个独立样本。小验证集会让指标变化看起来比真实更大。重尾损失会让平均值不稳定。概率提供了一套语言，让我们追问一个数字到底含有多少证据。

随机变量还帮助我们区分不同层级的波动。单个标签是随机的，因为样本可能含糊；训练集是随机的，因为它来自抽样；拟合出的模型是随机的，因为它依赖训练集和优化过程；评估指标也是随机的，因为它在有限测试集上计算。若把这些层级混在一起，人们就很容易把测量噪声误当成模型进步，或者把模型不稳定误认为数据本身不可预测。

03 / 条件化

条件概率、独立性与贝叶斯公式

条件概率是在证据中学习的语法。模型几乎从不询问一个事件孤立发生的概率；它询问的是给定特征后的标签概率、给定前文后的下一个 token 概率，或给定观测后的隐藏状态概率。独立性假设决定哪些信息可以拆开处理，哪些必须一起建模。贝叶斯公式则完成这幅图景：它说明先验信念和观察证据如何合成为后验信念。

条件概率是更新信息的数学。\(P(A\mid B)\) 询问在知道 \(B\) 已发生之后，事件 \(A\) 有多可能。在机器学习中，几乎所有有用陈述都是条件陈述：给定特征的标签概率、给定前文的下一个 token 概率、给定观察的隐藏原因概率、给定群体的失败概率。条件化把原始不确定性变成依赖上下文的信念。

定义为

\[P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)},\qquad P(B)>0\](2)

这个公式看起来简单，概念上意味着我们把注意力限制在 \(B\) 为真的世界里，并重新归一化。条件概率本身不是因果。\(P(Y\mid X)\) 描述数据分布中的关联，并不必然说明如果我们主动改变 \(X\) 会发生什么。因果推理需要普通条件化之外的额外假设。

独立与条件独立

当 \(P(A,B)=P(A)P(B)\) 时，事件 \(A\) 和 \(B\) 独立。随机变量独立时，联合分布可以分解。条件独立表示在观察某个变量之后，另外两个变量变得独立。在建模中，条件独立极其宝贵：它降低复杂度、允许因式分解，并让推断成为可能。朴素贝叶斯、隐马尔可夫模型、贝叶斯网络和许多图模型，都是通过声明哪些依赖重要、哪些可忽略来构建的。

独立性假设通常是近似而非真理。朴素贝叶斯假设特征在给定类别后条件独立，这对文本和图像几乎从不完全正确。但它仍可能很好用，因为即使概率模型不完美，决策边界也可能有用。评价概率模型时必须看任务：密度估计、校准预测、排序、分类或因果解释，对模型逼真度的要求并不相同。

贝叶斯公式是信念修正

贝叶斯公式来自联合概率的对称性：

\[P(H\mid D)=\frac{P(D\mid H)P(H)}{P(D)}\](3)

先验 \(P(H)\) 表示看到数据前的信念，似然 \(P(D\mid H)\) 表示假设与数据的相容程度，证据 \(P(D)\) 在假设之间归一化，后验 \(P(H\mid D)\) 是更新后的信念。在机器学习中，\(H\) 可以是类别、参数值、潜在簇、模型族或隐藏状态。贝叶斯公式是诊断、分类、滤波和贝叶斯推断的共同语法。

最常见错误是混淆 \(P(D\mid H)\) 和 \(P(H\mid D)\)。疾病会让检测呈阳性的概率很高，但如果疾病非常罕见，阳性检测后患病概率仍可能不高。基础率很重要。这不是哲学细节，而会决定垃圾邮件过滤、医学筛查、欺诈检测和任何部署在类别不平衡环境中的分类器。

在监督学习中，条件化也澄清了模型被允许知道什么。预测支付时欺诈概率，如果特征包含“是否已退款”这类结果之后才知道的信息，就属于错误的条件分布。它可能制造极高离线分数，却部署无用。正确的概率问题必须带有时间：在决策时刻可用信息给定时，目标发生的概率是多少？

04 / 分布

常见分布及其编码的建模假设

分布不是公式清单。每一种分布都是关于“哪些结果可能出现、我们预期怎样的随机性”的紧凑陈述。Bernoulli 分布表示事件是二值的，categorical 分布表示只能选择一个类别，高斯分布表示围绕中心的偏差对称且尾部较轻，Poisson 分布表示计数来自某种发生率。因此选择分布，就是在选择关于数据生成过程的故事。

分布是可复用的假设。选择 Bernoulli 分布，表示观察是二值的；选择 categorical 分布，表示多个类别中恰有一个发生；选择 Gaussian 分布，表示偏差连续、对称，并由均值和协方差概括；选择 Poisson 分布，表示计数来自某个率。分布名称是对支持集、噪声和变异方式的一段简写故事。

机器学习常把这些假设藏在损失函数里。平方误差对应常方差高斯观测噪声；分类交叉熵对应 categorical 似然；Poisson 回归建模计数；logistic 回归建模 Bernoulli 条件概率。理解损失背后的分布，能帮助我们判断损失何时合适，何时会扭曲问题。

离散分布

Bernoulli、binomial、categorical、multinomial 分别处理二值事件、重复二值试验、单个类别标签和多类别计数。文本模型在词表上使用 categorical 分布。文档朴素贝叶斯可能使用 multinomial 词频。推荐系统可能把点击建成 Bernoulli 事件。每一种情形中，分布都规定了哪些结果可能出现，以及概率如何求和为一。

categorical 分布在深度学习中尤其核心。分类器把特征映射成 logits，softmax 把 logits 转成类别概率，交叉熵比较预测 categorical 分布与观察标签。语言模型在每个位置对词表做同样事情。因此，分类和下一个 token 预测都是分布预测问题，而不仅仅是标签选择。

连续分布

高斯分布无处不在，因为它数学方便、可作为许多和的近似，并与最小二乘紧密相连。多元高斯 \(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma)\) 由均值和协方差塑造。对角协方差假设坐标噪声独立；完整协方差可以建模相关性，但需要更多数据。许多算法最容易理解为高斯假设加线性代数。

当高斯假设失效时，其他连续分布很重要。Exponential 分布建模等待时间，Gamma 分布建模正的率或方差，Beta 分布建模概率，Dirichlet 分布建模 categorical 概率向量，Student-\(t\) 分布处理更重的尾部。选择分布也是尊重约束：概率必须在零和一之间，方差必须为正，计数不能为负。

指数族与充分统计量

许多常用分布属于指数族：

\[p(x\mid\eta)=h(x)\exp\left(\eta^\top T(x)-A(\eta)\right)\](4)

其中 \(T(x)\) 是充分统计量，\(\eta\) 是自然参数，\(A(\eta)\) 是保证归一化的 log-partition function。这个形式统一了 Bernoulli、categorical、Gaussian、Poisson、Gamma 等分布，也解释了为什么对数似然梯度经常是在比较“观察到的统计量”和“模型期望的统计量”。

分布选择应跟被建模对象一致。一个限制在一到五之间的评分，不应自动当成无界高斯变量；等待时间不应由可能输出负数的模型预测；概率向量应生活在单纯形上。很多糟糕模型从忽略支持集约束开始，然后再试图在事后修补不可能的预测。概率建模的第一步，就是尊重变量能取什么值。

05 / 估计

似然、最大似然、MAP 与贝叶斯后验推断

估计是概率变成训练的地方。我们提出一个模型族，观察数据，然后询问哪些参数能让这些观察更合理。最大似然把这个问题转化为目标函数；MAP 加入先验偏好；贝叶斯推断则保留一整个“仍然合理的参数分布”，而不是只选一个点。实践上的教训是：机器学习中许多损失函数并不是随意惩罚，它们来自关于数据如何生成的概率假设。

估计关心如何从数据中学习未知量。设数据 \(D=\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n\) 由 \(p_\theta(x)\) 建模。似然 \(p_\theta(D)\) 把数据视为固定，把参数视为被评价的变量。如果样本独立，似然可以因式分解：

\[\mathcal{L}(\theta;D)=\prod_{i=1}^n p_\theta(x_i),\qquad \ell(\theta)=\sum_{i=1}^n\log p_\theta(x_i)\](5)

我们通常优化对数似然，因为许多小概率相乘会数值下溢，而求和更容易微分。最大似然估计选择让观察数据在模型下最可能的参数。很多学习算法都可以理解为在某个概率模型下做 MLE。

MLE 把概率与损失函数连接起来

带平方误差的线性回归，是高斯噪声假设下的 MLE；logistic 回归，是带 logistic link 的 Bernoulli 标签 MLE；softmax 分类，是 categorical 标签的 MLE；语言模型预训练，是自回归 categorical 分布的 MLE。这个连接很强，因为它把看似任意的损失函数转化为关于数据如何生成的假设。

当模型正确且数据充足时，MLE 可能统计效率很高；但参数灵活时也容易过拟合。模型可以给训练数据很高似然，却泛化很差。正则化、先验、验证、早停和数据增强，都是在修改估计问题，防止“拟合观察数据”退化为“记住观察偶然性”。

MAP 估计加入先验

最大后验估计选择后验概率最高的参数：

\[\theta_{\text{MAP}}=\arg\max_\theta\left[\log p(D\mid\theta)+\log p(\theta)\right]\](6)

先验 \(p(\theta)\) 表示看到数据之前的偏好。权重上的高斯先验对应 \(L_2\) 正则化；Laplace 先验对应类似 \(L_1\) 的稀疏压力。MAP 形式上是贝叶斯的，但仍然返回一个点估计。完整贝叶斯推断保留后验分布，用参数不确定性代替单一取值承诺。

后验预测分布

贝叶斯预测分布会在后验不确定性上平均：

\[p(y_\ast\mid x_\ast,D)=\int p(y_\ast\mid x_\ast,\theta)p(\theta\mid D)\,d\theta\](7)

这个公式很漂亮，也常常不可精确计算。某些共轭模型可以精确积分，但深度网络通常不行。近似推断、采样、变分方法、Laplace 近似、集成和 dropout 风格近似，都试图捕捉其中一部分不确定性。目标不只是预测标签，而是知道系统应该多信任自己。

因此，估计应被看成一条管线：选择概率故事，在某种抽样过程中收集数据，定义似然，加入正则或先验，优化或推断，再在尽量模拟部署的数据上检查预测行为。如果其中任何一步错配，最终参数估计可能看起来很精确，却回答了错误问题。概率让估计始终和产生数据的故事绑在一起。

06 / 分类

贝叶斯分类器、生成式模型、判别式模型与校准

一旦把三件事分开，分类会清楚很多：估计概率、选择标签、采取行动。贝叶斯分类器描述了后验概率和错误代价已知时的理想决策规则。生成式分类器建模每个类别如何生成特征；判别式分类器直接建模边界或后验；校准则检查输出概率能否被当成频率来信任。真实系统需要这三种视角，因为一个高分标签并不自动等于一个好决策。

分类是在不确定性下做决策。我们观察特征 \(x\)，选择标签 \(y\)。在零一损失下，理想分类器会选择后验概率最大的类别：

\[\hat{y}(x)=\arg\max_y P(Y=y\mid X=x)\](8)

这就是贝叶斯分类器。如果真实后验已知且所有错误代价相同，它是最优的。真实模型只能从有限数据近似它。当错误代价不相等时，决策论会把最大后验规则替换成最小期望损失规则。在医疗、欺诈、内容审核和安全系统中，阈值应反映代价，而不只是准确率。

从几何上看，贝叶斯分类器会把特征空间划分成若干区域，每个区域由某个后验概率占优的类别控制。两个类别之间的边界，是它们后验概率相等的点集。如果各类别条件分布是协方差共享的高斯分布，边界通常是线性的；如果协方差不同，边界会变成二次曲面。神经分类器可以形成更灵活的边界，但目标仍然是在逼近同一个后验排序：特征空间的这个位置，哪个标签应获得最多概率质量？

先验 \(P(Y=y)\) 表示观察特征之前每个类别的基础概率质量，在类别不平衡时尤其重要。如果某种疾病很罕见，即使症状模式看起来可疑，也必须克服很小的先验；如果欺诈本来很少，一个分类器可能频繁预测“非欺诈”而获得很高准确率。先验并不只是哲学立场，它决定证据如何转化为后验 odds；当部署人群发生变化时，即使 \(p(x\mid y)\) 近似不变，正确先验也可能需要重新估计。

贝叶斯分类器还把“排序”和“行动”分开。模型可能很会排列风险高低，但概率校准很差；也可能在某个阈值下分类很好，换一个阈值就失败。因此实际分类不应只报告准确率，还应报告混淆矩阵、ROC/PR 曲线、校准误差、子群指标和特定阈值下的期望代价。概率把分类从一个单一分数，变成可以检查、质疑并根据错误后果调整的决策曲面。

朴素贝叶斯与错误假设的价值

朴素贝叶斯使用贝叶斯公式，并假设特征在给定类别后条件独立：

\[P(Y=y\mid x_1,\ldots,x_d)\propto P(Y=y)\prod_{j=1}^{d}P(x_j\mid Y=y)\](9)

这个假设通常是假的，尤其在文本中词与词之间高度相关。但方法仍可能表现很好，因为它估计的参数少，且常常能正确排列类别。这给建模一个重要教训：一个简化概率模型可能不是世界的真实描述，却仍对决策有用。危险在于把它输出的概率过度解释成校准真理。

生成式分类器与判别式分类器

生成式分类器建模 \(p(x\mid y)\) 和 \(p(y)\)，再用贝叶斯公式得到 \(p(y\mid x)\)。判别式分类器则直接建模 \(p(y\mid x)\)，或直接学习决策边界而不建模特征生成。朴素贝叶斯和高斯判别分析是生成式的；logistic 回归和许多神经分类器是判别式的。生成式模型能利用未标注结构，也更自然处理缺失数据；判别式模型在标注数据充足时常有更好分类表现。

这个区分并非绝对。现代系统常把二者结合：生成式语言模型产生候选答案，判别式 reranker 评分，校准分类器决定是否拒答。概率问题始终相同：正在建模的是哪个分布，而这个分布是否正是当前决策需要的分布？

校准与决策阈值

如果模型在给出 0.8 概率的一组样本中大约有 80% 正确，它就是校准的。校准不同于准确率。高准确模型可能过度自信，较低准确模型也可能诚实表达不确定性。当概率驱动分诊、排序、风险分或人工复核时，校准非常重要。温度缩放、isotonic regression、Platt scaling 和基于验证集的阈值调优都是实用工具。

类别不平衡让校准和阈值更加关键。在罕见事件检测中，高准确率可能毫无意义，因为永远预测多数类已经看起来不错。Precision、recall、ROC 曲线、PR 曲线、期望代价和分组校准能提供更完整图景。概率有价值，是因为它让评分和决策分离：先估计风险，再根据正确代价模型选择行动。

贝叶斯分类器也说明，同一个分数在不同产品里可能对应不同动作。5% 的欺诈概率也许足以要求额外验证，却不足以直接阻断支付；5% 的患病概率如果疾病严重、复查便宜，就可能足以安排下一次检测。后验概率只是决策输入；损失函数、可用干预和对人的影响共同完成最终规则。

07 / 信息

熵、交叉熵、KL 散度与互信息

信息论给我们一套语言，用来比较模型预测前后的不确定性。熵衡量分布有多分散，交叉熵衡量用错误分布进行预测的代价，KL 散度衡量两个分布之间的不匹配，互信息衡量一个变量能减少多少关于另一个变量的不确定性。这些量在机器学习中无处不在，因为学习经常就是把概率质量移动到对数据更好的解释上。

信息论度量不确定性和分布不匹配。熵刻画随机变量的平均惊讶程度：

\[H(P)=-\sum_x P(x)\log P(x)\](10)

确定变量熵为零，因为没有惊讶；许多结果等可能的均匀分布熵较高，因为很多结果都合理。在机器学习中，熵出现在决策树、主动学习、探索、正则化、生成建模和不确定性可视化中。

交叉熵作为预测损失

真实分布 \(P\) 与模型分布 \(Q\) 的交叉熵是 \(H(P,Q)=-\mathbb{E}_{x\sim P}\log Q(x)\)。分类损失就是把观察标签看成 one-hot 经验分布后的交叉熵；语言模型损失则是在每个 token 位置上的交叉熵。最小化交叉熵鼓励模型给观察结果高概率，同时也会强烈惩罚在错误结果上的过度自信。

交叉熵不只是方便的损失。它也表示当数据来自 \(P\)，却使用为 \(Q\) 优化的编码时所需的额外 nats 或 bits。这个压缩视角解释了为什么预测建模和表示学习相互连接。能给真实数据更短编码的模型，捕捉到了数据生成过程中的结构。

KL 散度与分布不匹配

KL 散度为

\[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q)=\sum_x P(x)\log\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}\](11)

它衡量当数据来自 \(P\) 却使用 \(Q\) 时的代价。KL 非负但不对称，因此方向很重要。变分推断、扩散模型、强化学习策略更新、语言模型对齐和领域自适应都使用 KL 或类似惩罚。不同方向会产生不同行为：一个方向可能覆盖所有模式，另一个方向可能集中在单个高密度模式。

互信息与有用表示

互信息衡量知道一个变量能减少多少另一个变量的不确定性。好的特征表示应保留关于目标的信息，同时丢弃无关扰动。这一思想出现在表示学习、对比学习、特征选择和信息瓶颈方法中。困难在于，高维互信息很难可靠估计，因此实际算法常使用下界和代理目标。

信息论语言很强，但如果脱离估计器和假设，也会变得含糊。当有人说模型“最大化信息”时，应追问是哪两个变量、哪个分布、哪个估计器、哪个界。否则，这句话可能遮蔽的东西比解释的更多。好的概率实践会让可测量的量始终可见。

信息论还直观解释了正则化。一个保留输入所有细节的表示，可能把无关扰动也记住；一个压缩过度的表示，又无法预测目标。学习常常是在寻找一种压缩：保留有用 bits，丢弃无关 bits。这就是为什么噪声注入、瓶颈、对比目标和最小描述长度思想会以不同名字反复出现。

08 / 图模型

图模型、潜变量与概率结构

这一节展示概率如何处理很多变量，同时不丢失问题形状。图模型是一张依赖假设地图：它说明哪些变量直接影响哪些变量，以及在给定某些变量后哪些关系可以变得独立。潜变量加入不可直接观察的隐藏原因，使观察到的模式更容易解释。目的不是画漂亮图，而是把假设说得足够明确，让推断、学习和批判成为可能。

图模型用图表达因式分解和条件独立。节点是随机变量，边表示模型中的直接概率依赖。有向贝叶斯网络把联合分布分解成局部条件分布：

\[p(x_1,\ldots,x_d)=\prod_{j=1}^{d}p(x_j\mid \operatorname{pa}(x_j))\](12)

这种分解可以把不可管理的联合分布拆成可处理的局部片段，也让假设显式化。如果一个节点只有少数父节点，模型就声明其他变量只能通过这些父节点影响它。这是模块化设计在概率中的对应物。

潜变量解释隐藏结构

潜变量是用于解释观察数据的未观察原因或摘要。混合模型使用隐藏簇分配，主题模型使用隐藏主题比例，隐马尔可夫模型使用隐藏状态，因子分析使用隐藏连续因子。潜变量有用，是因为它们允许模型用较简单的隐藏结构解释复杂观察，但也带来可识别性和推断困难。

在机器学习中，潜变量常像压缩解释。簇标签概括一个群体，表示向量概括一个输入，状态变量概括历史。概率问题是：潜变量是否有清晰生成角色，它是否能从数据中识别，下游决策是否需要解释它，还是只需把它作为预测中间量使用？

EM 算法：在隐藏分配和参数之间交替

Expectation-Maximization 算法通过两个步骤处理含潜变量模型。E 步在当前参数下计算或近似隐藏变量后验；M 步更新参数以最大化期望完整数据对数似然。对高斯混合模型而言，这意味着先估计软簇成员资格，再更新簇均值、协方差和权重。

EM 不是魔法，也不保证找到全局最优。它可能陷入局部最优，依赖初始化，并在组件过多时过拟合。它的价值在于概念：当数据有隐藏结构时，学习常常在“用隐藏变量解释观察”和“改进解释模型”之间交替。变分推断和许多现代潜变量模型都推广了这一思想。

概率编程与模型透明性

概率编程语言允许用户写出生成模型，并把推断交给算法。这把模型说明与推断机制分离。在流行病学、预测、因果建模、科学测量和决策支持中，当领域假设重要时，这类工具非常有用。它们让不确定性显式可检查，但仍要求认真检查先验、似然、收敛和模型拟合。

深度学习常自动学习表示，图模型则要求我们指定结构。二者互补。神经网络可以作为图模型中条件分布的参数化器；概率模型也可以在神经模块外加入结构。前沿方向不是二选一，而是把灵活函数近似与显式不确定性、依赖结构结合起来。

图模型思维对调试泄漏和伪相关尤其有用。把数据来源、标注过程、用户群体、时间、特征、标签和预测都画成节点，常能暴露本不该存在的路径。如果医院 ID 能预测疾病，是因为某医院服务了更重症患者，那么图能帮助区分真实医疗信号和部署时脆弱的捷径。图本身不能解决问题，但能让假设可见。

09 / 推断

采样、Monte Carlo、MCMC 与变分推断

很多概率问题无法通过闭式代数直接求解。采样方法用模拟回答：抽取许多可能世界，然后平均它们的行为。MCMC 通过精心设计的随机游走，从复杂分布中构造样本。变分推断则采用另一条路线：用一个更简单的分布替代困难分布，并优化这个近似。两种策略都很重要，因为现代机器学习常常需要不确定性估计，即使精确推断不可行。

许多概率问题需要计算无法精确得到的期望或后验分布。积分可能是高维的，归一化常数可能未知，模型也可能太复杂。近似推断就是用受控近似替代不可能的精确计算。机器学习在贝叶斯模型、生成模型、强化学习、不确定性估计和模拟中都使用这些方法。

Monte Carlo 方法用样本近似期望：

\[\mathbb{E}_{p}[f(X)]\approx \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S}f(x^{(s)}),\qquad x^{(s)}\sim p\](13)

在广泛条件下，近似误差按 \(1/\sqrt{S}\) 下降，形式上与维度无关；但在高维中获得好样本可能非常困难。因此，采样质量不只是样本数更重要。相关或有偏样本会让估计看起来精确却错误。

MCMC 从只差常数的分布中采样

Markov Chain Monte Carlo 构造一个平稳分布为目标后验的马尔可夫链。当我们能计算未归一化密度，却不能计算归一化常数时，它非常有用。Metropolis-Hastings 提出移动，并用接受概率修正 proposal；Hamiltonian Monte Carlo 使用梯度信息，在连续参数空间中更高效移动。

MCMC 诊断很重要。链可能混合缓慢，被困在某个模式中，或看起来稳定却错过重要区域。多条链、有效样本量、轨迹图和领域检查都能帮助判断。核心教训是：采样本身也是带不确定性的计算。输出不是单个数字，而是一个需要评估可靠性的近似。

变分推断把推断变成优化

变分推断选择一个近似族 \(q_\phi(z)\)，并寻找其中最接近真实后验的成员，常通过最大化 evidence lower bound 实现。它通常比 MCMC 更快、更可扩展，但会引入近似偏差。近似族可能太简单，从而低估不确定性。在深度学习中，VAE、amortized inference 和许多潜变量模型都依赖变分思想。

MCMC 与变分推断的取舍反映了更普遍模式。采样可能更忠实但昂贵，优化可能可扩展但有偏。实际 ML 系统通常选择“对当前决策足够好”的近似。如果不确定性决定医疗分诊或科学结论，近似质量就比只用于低风险推荐排序时重要得多。

模拟也是概率思维的一种形式。部署前，我们可以模拟标签噪声、缺失数据、类别不平衡、漂移、延迟反馈或罕见事件，然后观察训练管线如何反应。模拟不能证明现实一定如此，但它是一个受控实验室，可以发现系统默默依赖了哪些假设，以及这些假设被破坏时会怎样。

10 / 生成

生成模型：从混合模型到 VAE、自回归与扩散

生成模型是给可能数据分配概率质量的模型，而不仅仅给标签分配概率。问题由“这属于哪一类”变成“哪些对象可能存在，它们有多可能”。混合模型组合简单成分，VAE 引入连续潜在原因，自回归模型一次生成一个部分，扩散模型学习反转噪声。不同架构选择了不同折中：似然、样本质量、可控性和计算可行性之间没有免费午餐。

生成建模试图学习数据上的分布。分类器估计 \(p(y\mid x)\)，生成模型则试图表示 \(p(x)\)、\(p(x,y)\) 或 \(p(x\mid c)\) 等条件分布。这允许采样、补全、去噪、模拟、压缩、异常检测和条件创造。评价生成模型时，不只看它是否预测标签，还看它是否捕捉数据本身的结构。

较早的生成模型包括高斯混合、隐马尔可夫模型、主题模型和因子分析。它们依然有价值，因为假设显式，推断可解释。现代神经生成模型用深度网络参数化分布，同时保留概率目标。词汇变了，但核心问题仍然是：概率质量如何分配到可能观察上？

自回归视角解释了为什么 LLM 是概率生成器。它们不是从表中取出固定答案，而是不断从条件分布采样。温度、top-\(p\) 等解码设置会改变概率如何转化为文本。因此，可靠性取决于学习到的分布、提示和采样策略。

VAE 与潜变量生成

变分自编码器引入潜变量 \(z\)、解码器 \(p_\theta(x\mid z)\) 和近似编码器 \(q_\phi(z\mid x)\)。训练会最大化一个下界，在重建质量和把潜代码拉近先验的 KL 惩罚之间平衡。结果是一个平滑潜空间，可以从中生成新样本。VAE 让概率模型显式，但在某些领域中样本可能比对抗模型或扩散模型更模糊。

当观察背后有隐藏因素时，潜变量很有用：风格、主题、姿态、说话人、簇或环境。好的潜变量模型会把有意义变化与噪声分开。但 disentanglement 不会靠愿望自动发生，它依赖架构、目标、监督、归纳偏置和数据。概率告诉我们正在优化什么，却不能保证潜轴天然对应人类概念。

扩散模型作为学习到的去噪过程

扩散模型逐步向数据加噪，再训练神经网络反转这个过程。生成从噪声开始，反复去噪。概率上，模型学习一系列从简单先验分布走向数据分布的转移。这一框架已经成为图像、音频、视频和越来越多多模态生成的核心方法。

扩散强调一个反复出现的主题：复杂分布可以被拆成许多简单条件步骤来学习。自回归模型逐 token 生成，扩散模型逐步去噪，normalizing flow 通过可逆映射变换简单分布。不同生成模型家族选择了不同方式，让概率计算变得可处理。

生成模型也必须按用途评估。如果目标是真实图像，感知质量和多样性重要；如果目标是异常检测，似然排序和误报率重要；如果目标是科学模拟，校准不确定性和守恒规律可能比视觉吸引力更重要。模型可能生成惊艳样本却给出糟糕概率，也可能似然很好但样本无趣。分布问题必须匹配使用场景。

11 / 不确定性

深度学习中的不确定性：偶然、认知、集成与 conformal prediction

深度网络常输出很自信的数字，但自信不等于可信的不确定性。偶然不确定性来自即使知道一切也仍然存在的噪声；认知不确定性来自数据有限或模型不确定。集成方法近似“多个合理模型”的思想，conformal prediction 则在较少建模假设下提供覆盖率式保证。实践中的关键问题是：系统什么时候应该预测，什么时候应该扩大答案范围，什么时候应该请求帮助。

深度网络经常输出概率，但 softmax 分数并不自动等于可信不确定性。模型可能对分布外输入、对抗样本、损坏传感器或训练中代表不足的群体过度自信。因此，概率在深度学习中有两项工作：定义训练目标，以及在部署中表达不确定性。第二项更难，因为它要求模型知道自己不知道什么。

Aleatoric uncertainty 是数据中的不可约噪声：模糊图片、随机用户行为或确实含糊的标签。Epistemic uncertainty 是由于数据有限或知识不足造成的模型不确定性。更多数据可以减少认知不确定性，却不能消除偶然不确定性。区分二者很重要：如果不确定性来自数据噪声，系统可能需要人工复核或更好传感器；如果来自认知不足，则可能需要该区域更多数据。

集成与近似贝叶斯思维

集成训练多个模型并比较预测。预测一致表示稳定，不一致提示不确定。深度集成简单且常很强，但成本较高。推理时 dropout、Laplace 近似、stochastic weight averaging 和贝叶斯神经网络，都试图更直接或更便宜地表示参数不确定性。它们都不完美，但都帮助我们走出单一过度自信预测。

实际问题是部署分布上的校准。一个在验证集校准的集成，分布漂移后仍可能失准。监控不应只看平均准确率，还要看置信度、分组表现、漂移和拒答行为。概率输出是一种关于频率的承诺；生产数据会检验这个承诺是否兑现。

Conformal prediction 提供较少分布假设的覆盖保证

Conformal prediction 在 exchangeability 条件下构造具有覆盖保证的预测集合。对分类器来说，它不只返回一个标签，而是返回一组可能标签。模型越确定，集合越小；越不确定，集合越大。保证关注长期覆盖率，不保证每个单独集合一定包含真值。它有吸引力，因为可以包裹许多已有模型。

和所有保证一样，conformal prediction 依赖假设。如果校准数据和未来数据不可交换，覆盖可能失败；如果群体差异明显，平均覆盖可能隐藏某些子群覆盖不足。即便如此，这个方法体现了很好的概率习惯：当单个预测不够可靠时，返回一个下游决策可使用的结构化不确定性陈述。

分布外检测与拒答

在一个分布上训练的模型可能面对另一个分布。OOD 检测试图识别与训练数据差异足够大的输入，使其预测不应被信任。方法包括密度分数、特征空间距离、energy score、集成、重建误差和专门检测器。难点在于，高维密度和语义新颖性并不相同：看起来奇怪的输入可能无害，看起来自然的输入也可能离开任务分布。

拒答是一种决策，不是丢脸。知道何时延迟判断的系统，可能比永远回答的系统更安全。概率通过估计置信度、期望代价和信息价值支持拒答。在高风险 ML 中，最佳行动可能是索要更多数据、调用工具、转给人类，或拒绝做出判断。

不确定性还必须被正确沟通。用户未必知道 0.73 的分数意味着什么，却可能理解“高风险，需要复核”或“预测集合包含三个可能标签”。界面既要保留概率含义，又不能假装数字比数据所支持的更精确。好的不确定性设计同时是统计问题和人与系统交互问题。

12 / 实践

评估、泄漏、公平性与概率机器学习检查表

最后一节把概率变成工程纪律。概率模型不能只用准确率评价，因为部署决策依赖校准、不确定性、子群表现、分布漂移和不同错误的代价。评估要问的是：训练时给出的概率陈述，在未来环境中是否仍然保持同样含义？这时，数据泄漏、公平性、监控和决策阈值就不再是分散话题，而是同一个概率工作流的组成部分。

概率真正有用，是因为它改变我们评估系统的方式。准确率通常远远不够。我们需要不确定性区间、校准曲线、代价敏感指标、分组指标、漂移监控和错误分析。模型部署后面对的是未来案例流，而不是训练表格。评估应尽量近似系统将支持的决策和将面对的人群。

数据泄漏是一种概率失败：来自未来、验证集或目标的信息进入训练特征，使评估分布比部署分布更容易。泄漏可能来自预处理、重复记录、时间窗口、由目标派生的特征、用户级切分或全局统计量。解决方法是在建模前定义预测时间、可用信息、抽样单位和切分规则。

贯穿机器学习工作流的概率视角

一个完整的机器学习项目，可以被看成一串条件概率问题。数据收集阶段要问样本代表哪个总体：\(p_{\text{train}}(x,y)\) 从来不是世界本身，只是由传感器、日志规则、平台激励、缺失机制和选择偏差共同暴露出来的那一部分世界。特征构造阶段要问哪些变量在决策前可见，哪些变量其实泄漏了未来信息。建模阶段要问哪一种条件分布足够有用：预测任务常关心 \(p(y\mid x)\)，生成和诊断任务常关心 \(p(x,y)\)，存在隐藏结构时还要推断 \(p(z\mid x)\)。训练阶段要问什么准则能让提出的分布接近经验证据，部署阶段则要问这些条件关系离开数据集之后是否仍然成立。

这个视角解释了为什么概率论不是一个孤立的先修章节，而是整个系统的语法。回归模型通常估计条件均值 \(E[Y\mid X=x]\)，同时隐含均值周围的噪声假设。分类器估计或近似类别概率，再通过阈值把概率转成行动。推荐系统从稀疏观察中估计不确定的偏好。强化学习策略从依赖状态的分布中采样动作，并用延迟奖励更新分布。语言模型估计下一个 token 的分布，解码过程再把这个分布变成一段具体文本。不同领域看起来不同，是因为对象不同；但重复出现的模式相同：观察不完整证据，表示不确定性，更新信念，并在代价约束下选择行动。

几何和概率也会以非常具体的方式相遇。向量上的分布是一团可能点构成的云；均值是这团云的质心，协方差椭球描述主要波动方向，密度等高线标出同等可信的区域。学习会改变这种几何。PCA 把坐标系旋转到高方差方向；高斯判别分析比较不同类别的椭球；logistic 回归把线性分数通过 sigmoid 弯成概率；神经网络反复变换数据云，直到最终表示能更容易表达有用的条件概率。因此概率的物理图像不只是“随机”，还包括概率质量在表示空间中的移动、压缩、分离与重新分配。

在训练过程中，概率假设会变成梯度。负对数似然意味着被模型赋予低概率的样本会产生更大的纠正力量。交叉熵把概率质量推向观测标签。KL 散度惩罚一个分布把质量放在另一个分布认为不应出现的位置。变分推断和扩散模型训练优化的下界、去噪损失，来源上都是概率对象，但实现时表现为普通的可微目标函数。这也是为什么现代机器学习中概率与优化无法完全分开：概率定义“什么应当为真”，优化则给出参数如何移动到那里的路径。

概率还让“预测”和“决策”的区别更加清楚。概率 \(0.8\) 本身不是行动；只有在指定效用、代价、约束和额外信息价值之后，它才会变成行动。在医疗分诊中，因为漏掉危险病例代价很高，较低阈值可能更合理。在内容排序中，校准和长期反馈可能比单步准确率更重要。在欺诈检测中，一个预测可能触发人工复核，而不是自动拒绝。决策论把这一点写成明确形式：选择使条件期望风险 \(E[L(a,Y)\mid x]\) 最小的动作 \(a\)，而不只是选择概率最大的标签。

最后，部署会把每一个概率模型变成监控问题。训练分布是历史的，部署分布却是活的。用户行为会变，数据管道会变，传感器会漂移，政策会改变激励，模型自己的决策也可能重塑未来数据。因此概率机器学习系统需要漂移检查、校准监控、不确定性摘要、子群分析和人工升级路径。真正实用的问题不是“模型是否曾经拟合过一个数据集”，而是“在什么条件下，它给出的概率陈述仍然足够可信，可以支撑绑定在其后的决策”。

公平性与子群不确定性

模型可以平均表现很好，却在某个子群表现很差。概率通过估计每个群体指标及其不确定性，并分别检查校准，帮助揭示这一点。但公平性标准可能冲突：equalized odds、demographic parity、predictive parity 和 calibration 不一定能同时满足。正确标准取决于决策场景和社会代价，而不只是数学形式。

小子群会带来宽不确定区间。测得的差距可能只是噪声，真实差距也可能因数据不足被隐藏。负责任评估同时报告估计和不确定性，也检查数据收集、标签质量、历史偏差和反馈循环。概率不能替我们决定价值，但可以防止虚假的精确感。

概率与机器学习的学习地图

稳固路线应从样本空间、随机变量、期望、方差、条件化、贝叶斯公式和常见分布开始。之后进入似然、估计、贝叶斯推断、熵、KL 散度、图模型和采样。进入机器学习后，应把这些概念连接到分类、回归、校准、生成模型、不确定性估计和分布漂移下的评估。每个概念都应通过小型模拟练习，而不只是作为公式阅读。

核心习惯

每当模型输出一个数字，都应追问：这是关于什么的概率，条件化在哪些信息上，来自哪个模型，由哪些数据估计，并将用于什么决策？这个问题能把概率论从数学章节变成学习系统中的工程纪律。

实践学习应多做小实验。模拟有偏抽样，观察准确率如何失效；在同一文本数据上训练朴素贝叶斯和 logistic 回归，比较校准；拟合高斯混合并观察 EM 对初始化的敏感性；用 Monte Carlo 估计期望并画出误差随样本数变化；用温度缩放校准神经分类器。这些练习会让概率成为可观察行为，而不只是纸面公式。
